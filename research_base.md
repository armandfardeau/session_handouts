## 1. Sailing pedagogy fundamentals (FFVoile curriculum)

### Key learnings

- **FFVoile "Voile Croisière" structure**: Five progressive levels (1 to 5), each validated by a **Livret de Progression** (progress booklet) completed with an instructor.
  - **Niveau 1 (Initiation)** – First maneuvers in a secure environment, getting comfortable on board, basic maneuvers under instructor supervision.
  - **Niveau 2 (Autonomie encadrée)** – Supervised autonomy on a familiar boat.
  - **Niveau 3 (Autonomie)** – Acting as crew on a cruise.
  - **Niveau 4 (Performance)** – Acting as skipper on a cruise (instructor-supervised).
  - **Niveau 5 (Expertise)** – Skipper in complete autonomy, including night sailing.
- **Three "savoirs" (knowledge domains)** that organize every level:
  - **Savoir-faire technique** – maneuvers, sail handling, navigation.
  - **Savoir-être collectif** – collective behavior, communication, crew life.
  - **Savoir-être environnemental** – environmental awareness, weather, safety.
- **Pedagogical philosophy**: progression is competency-based (each skill is signed off in the booklet), not purely time-based. The instructor validates each item with the sailor present.
- **Level 1 typical skills**: get dressed (don life jacket, foul weather gear), recover a man overboard with a buoyant object, hold a heading under tow, secure a boat in port, tie basic knots, identify the main parts of the boat and sail.
- **Level 2 typical skills**: crew autonomy on a day sail — reefing under supervision, MOB with a halyard/boat hook, basic pilotage (compass + landmarks), understanding a marine weather bulletin.
- **Level 3 typical skills**: acting as crew on a multi-day cruise — night watches, sail changes without instructor help, anchoring under supervision.
- **Level 4 typical skills**: acting as skipper for a day's sail — full responsibility for the crew, boat, decisions; managing crew fatigue; passage planning.

- **Discover Boating — Learning the basics of sailing**: <https://www.discoverboating.com/resources/learning-the-basics-of-sailing>

### Practical teaching tips (FFVoile levels 1–4)

- **Always start with safety.** Before any "fun" content, the sailor must pass the Level 1 safety items (donning a life jacket in <60 s, recovering a buoyant MOB object).
- **Use the livret de progression as a checklist.** Each article tied to a skill should reference which livret item it covers — readers can self-locate their level.
- **Spiral learning.** Reinforce prior levels: a Level 3 article can revisit Level 1 knots and Level 2 weather reading without "talking down" to the reader.
- **Three pillars in every article.** Wherever possible, link technical content to crew life and environment — that's how FFVoile frames the whole journey.

## 2. Sailing techniques

### 2.1 Tacking (virement de bord)

- **Definition**: changing direction by turning the **bow through the wind**, so the wind passes over the stern-side of the boat to fill the sails on the opposite tack.
- **Why it matters**: it's how a sailing boat makes progress upwind — you can't sail straight into the wind; you zigzag ("beat") by tacking back and forth.
- **Standard maneuver sequence** (dinghy):
  1. Helm: "Ready about!" — alert the crew.
  2. Crew: "Ready!" — acknowledge; trimmer prepares the jib sheet.
  3. Helm: "Helm's alee!" — push the tiller to leeward, the bow turns into the wind.
  4. As the bow crosses head-to-wind, the **jib is eased (let fly)** and then **sheeted in on the new leeward side**.
  5. The mainsail follows, filling on the new tack.
  6. Helm: "Helm on the new side" / "Filled on both" once both sails are drawing.
- **Common errors**: helmsman turns too slowly (boat stalls head-to-wind), jib trimmer sheets in too early (back-winds the jib), crew stands up in the middle of the maneuver (capsize risk on dinghies).
- **No-go zone**: sailing directly into the wind — sails luff, the boat stalls. You must tack to make upwind progress.

### 2.2 Jibing (empannage)

- **Definition**: changing direction by turning the **stern through the wind**, so the wind crosses the stern of the boat to fill the sails on the opposite side.
- **Difference from tacking**: in jibing, the wind pushes on the **back of the sail**, so the mainsail flies across the boat. The boom can move fast and dangerously — always announce and watch for crew heads.
- **Standard maneuver sequence** (keelboat):
  1. Helm: "Prepare to jibe!" — alert crew.
  2. Crew: "Ready!"
  3. Mainsheet trimmer **eases the mainsheet** to control boom speed.
  4. Helm: "Jibe ho!" — turns the tiller toward the wind, the stern passes through the wind.
  5. Boom sweeps across; trimmer **trim the mainsheet on the new side** and **trim the preventer** if fitted.
  6. Helm: "Jibed!" once the boat is on the new gybe and sails are drawing.
- **Preventer**: a line rigged from the end of the boom forward to a deck fitting, holding the boom out and preventing an accidental gybe in stronger wind — strongly recommended above F5.
- **Risk**: a **gybe is the most dangerous routine maneuver** — accidental gybes cause serious injuries (boom to the head). Always announce, look around, and use a preventer offshore.

### 2.3 Sail trim

- **Three controls on the mainsail** (typical sloop):
  - **Mainsheet** — controls the angle of the boom and therefore the camber (curve) of the sail.
  - **Boom vang / kicking strap** — controls twist (top of the sail moving out vs. in).
  - **Outhaul / cunningham / halyard tension** — controls the sail's fore-and-aft shape (depth).
- **Tell-tales**: short yarn streamers on the sail. The windward tell-tale streaming cleanly means the sail is trimmed correctly; if the leeward tell-tale lifts first, the sail is **over-trimmed** (pinching the flow); if the windward stalls first, it's **under-trimmed** (luffing, loss of power).
- **Point of sail and trim**:
  - **Close-hauled (près serré)**: sails sheeted hard, tell-tales streaming.
  - **Beam reach (travers)**: sails eased ~45° from centerline, balanced heel.
  - **Broad reach (grand largue)**: sails eased well out, boat heel reduces.
  - **Run (vent arrière)**: sails let out almost to the shrouds, main often boomed out with a preventer or pole.

### 2.4 Boat balance (heel, hiking, crew position)

- **Heel** is the sideways tilt caused by the wind pushing on the sails.
- **Sweet-spot heel angle**: most cruising monohulls sail well at **15°–20° of heel**; above ~25°–30° the boat is "over-canvassed" — rudder loses grip, water laps the leeward rail, crew movement becomes unsafe.
- **Why it matters**: when the boat heels excessively, the rudder is no longer vertical and the hull shape changes — the boat **slides sideways** (leeway), the **helm feels "heavy"** (weather helm), and speed drops.
- **Hiking**: moving crew body weight **to windward** to counter heeling force.
  - **Dinghies** use **toe straps** / hiking straps; sailors sit on the windward side, feet hooked under the strap, torso leaning out.
  - **Performance dinghies and catamarans** use a **trapeze** — a wire the crew hangs from to lean out even further.
  - On larger **keelboats**, crew weight has less effect on righting moment than the keel itself, but **crew on the windward side still helps a few degrees** and improves comfort and morale on long passages.
- **Two key forces that decide helm feel**:
  - **Center of Effort (CE)** — where the wind pushes on the sails.
  - **Center of Lateral Resistance (CLR)** — where the keel and hull resist sideways motion.
  - When CE and CLR are aligned, the helm is balanced; if CE moves aft of CLR, you get **weather helm** (the boat wants to round up into the wind).

### 2.5 Steering (barre / gouvernail)

- **Steer by the wind**, not by the boat — keep the sails working. On a reach, the angle of the jib tell-tales is your best reference; trim and steer to keep them streaming.
- **Look ahead**, not at the bow. Pick a landmark or compass heading well in front of the boat, and steer toward it.
- **Small corrections** — sailboats respond with a delay. Over-correcting with the tiller creates an S-curve and costs speed. Anticipate the wave, the gust, the puff.
- **Helmsman's posture**: sit facing forward, both hands on the tiller (or wheel), feet braced, weight low and centered. Avoid gripping — a light touch steers more accurately.

### Practical teaching tips

- **Order of teaching**: vocabulary → points of sail → rope handling → tacking → jibing → sail trim → balance/hiking → more complex maneuvers (reefing, anchoring, MOB). Don't skip ahead.
- **Always teach the "why" before the "how"**: explain the no-go zone *before* tacking; explain boom swing *before* jibing.
- **Use role-play**: assign one student the role of helm, another the role of jib trimmer; rotate. The crew member's role teaches communication (the strongest predictor of a clean maneuver).
- **Tip for teaching balance**: ask the student to put their hand over the leeward rail — if the wind touches it, they're heeling too much. Then have them move to windward and notice the change.
- **Steering drill**: "red hand, red sail" — a beginner mnemonic on some boats: when the wind is coming over the right shoulder, the right hand tends to be the "active" trim hand.

## 3. Safety practices

### 3.1 Life jackets (gilets de sauvetage)

- **Always worn when on deck** — non-negotiable in FFVoile Level 1 onward. Many accidents occur during deck work, on slippery surfaces, or in cold water where cold-shock can incapacitate a swimmer within minutes.
- **Two main types**:
  - **Lifejacket** (gilet de sauvetage) — designed to turn an unconscious person face-up. Higher buoyancy (typically 150 N+).
  - **Buoyancy aid** (aide à la flottabilité) — helps a conscious swimmer stay afloat; **does NOT** guarantee face-up flotation.
- **Fitting**: the right size for the wearer's weight; the crotch strap (if fitted) must be done up, otherwise the jacket can ride up over the head.
- **Standards**: in Europe, look for **CE marking** and the **ISO 12402** rating; in the US/Canada, look for **USCG approval**. Check for the right activity — some jackets are not approved for sailing (e.g., fishing vests).
- **Maintenance check** (every outing): buckles, inflator (for inflatable types), expiry date for CO₂ cartridges, no rips, whistle attached.

### 3.2 Harnesses and tethers (longes)

- **When used**: offshore, in stronger wind, at night, when the crew is alone on deck — anything that increases the cost of going overboard.
- **Components**: harness worn over the body, tether clipped to the harness at one end and to a strong point on the boat at the other.
- **Best practice**:
  - **Two clip-in points** on deck (one forward, one aft) so the crew can move along the boat **always clipped in**.
  - **Short tether** for working at the mast or helm (less risk of swinging across the boat).
  - **Long tether** for moving between clip-in points.
  - **Triple hook** tethers allow quick changeover without going unclipped.
- **Jacklines** (lifelines along the deck) — strong webbing or line rigged fore-and-aft on each side of the deck so the crew has a continuous attachment point.

### 3.3 Man-overboard (MOB) recovery

- **Universal rule**: **point at the person, then act** — keeps eyes on the victim and alerts the rest of the crew.
- **Standard step-by-step** (cruising sailboat, engine available):
  1. **Shout "Man overboard!"** — loud, clear, once.
  2. **Point at the MOB** continuously — assign one crew member to keep eyes on them.
  3. **Deploy a buoyant aid** (horseshoe buoy, life ring, dan buoy) — even if the MOB is wearing a PFD, a visual marker speeds recovery.
  4. **Hit the MOB button** on the chartplotter/GPS if fitted — it records the position.
  5. **Approach**: bring the boat back under sail or power, sailing on a beam reach is often easiest; the goal is to come up alongside the MOB with the boat hove-to or stopped, **leeward of the person** so they drift toward you.
  6. **Recovery**: hoist the person over the leeward rail, or with a halyard and a boat hook (FFVoile Level 2 standard).
- **For dinghies** (FFVoile Level 1–2): recovery is much simpler — approach, get the sailor to grab the painter (bow line), help them back aboard from the windward side; right the boat if capsized by swimming to the centerboard and pulling on it.
- **Time is critical**: in cold water, survival depends on getting out within minutes. Practice the maneuver at least once per season.

### 3.4 Weather awareness

- **Beaufort scale**: 0 (calm) to 12 (hurricane) — relates wind speed to observed sea state. Sailors should know the scale by heart up to at least F7.
  - **F0–F2** (0–6 kn): light — good for Level 1 beginners.
  - **F3–F4** (7–16 kn): gentle to moderate — the bread and butter of Level 2 sailing.
  - **F5–F6** (17–27 kn): fresh to strong — reef early, Level 3+ only.
  - **F7+** (28+ kn): near gale and above — small craft should stay in harbor.
- **Reading the sky**:
  - **Cumulus building into towers** in the afternoon — possible thunderstorm gusts.
  - **Halo around the sun or moon** — high cirrus, fronts approaching.
  - **Sudden wind shift, drop in temperature, smell of rain** — gust front.
  - **Wind backing** (counter-clockwise in NH) — typically worsening weather.
  - **Wind veering** (clockwise in NH) — typically improving weather (in mid-latitudes).
- **Marine forecast essentials**: wind direction and force, gusts, sea state, swell, visibility, warnings (small craft, gale, storm), trend (building/ easing).
- **FFVoile Level 2 expectation**: sailor can read a marine bulletin and explain whether conditions match the planned outing.

### Practical teaching tips

- **Always demo the recovery from the boat's actual geometry.** A lecture on MOB is wasted unless the student has actually pulled a "victim" (a fender or a PFD weighted as a person) back aboard.
- **Make the Beaufort scale tactile.** Tie small flags to a hand-held anemometer at home, or use the "white horse on wave" rule: count whitecaps. One whitecap = ~F3, scattered = F4, many streaks = F5–F6.
- **"Point first, then act"** — drill it until it's automatic. Students who shout and turn away lose sight of the MOB in heavy weather.
- **PFD drills should be quick and embarrassing-free.** Have students don and doff in under 60 seconds while wet and cold. Practice regularly.
- **Harness hook-up in dry conditions first**, then in seaway, then at night. Add layers of difficulty slowly.
- **Weather briefings should be ritual.** Open every outing with a 5-minute weather call: what does the bulletin say, what does the sky say, what do we do if it changes?

## 4. Fun activities for sailors

### 4.1 Group exercises (school / club settings)

- **"Sail the school":** an instructor plays the wind; students must "trim" a classroom-sized mainsheet (a long rope) based on announced wind shifts. Teaches trim and call-and-response.
- **Knot relay races:** teams tie a bowline, figure-8, and clove hitch as fast as they can, with proper finish. Builds muscle memory and team spirit.
- **"Blind sail"**: one crew is blindfolded (or below deck), must follow helm commands to find a buoy by sound and feel. Builds trust and communication.
- **MOB race**: a fender is thrown, every crew must point, deploy a buoy, approach, and recover — timed. Fun and serious at the same time.
- **Triangle race drills**: short course set around three buoys; rotate crew positions; focus on maneuvers rather than winning.
- **Man overboard pole dance**: rotate the MOB pole between crews; the "MOB" pretends to struggle and provides feedback.

### 4.2 Racing formats

- **Fleet racing**: the most common. Boats start together (a sequence of warning signal, preparatory signal, and start signal at 5-4-1-0 minutes before the start), sail a defined course, finish in order.
- **Match racing**: two boats only, head-to-head, like a duel. Tactics focus on the start and the other boat's wind.
- **Team racing**: typically 3 vs 3, with the goal that the team's *lowest* score determines the team's score. Boats are usually sailed with crew from multiple teams.
- **Dinghy racing series**: Olympic-style classes (Laser, 470, 49er, Nacra 17, Finn, RS:X) and club classes (Optimist, RS Aero, ILCA, 420).
- **Cruising-class racing**: PHRF, IRC, ORC ratings — boats of different sizes race together using handicap time corrections.
- **Around-the-buoys race**: classic windward-leeward course, often with a reaching finish. Teaches starts, mark roundings, and finish tactics.
- **Pursuit race**: boats start at staggered times based on their handicap — first to the finish line wins. Great for beginners and mixed fleets because everyone is on the course at the same time.
- **Long-distance race**: daysailing, overnight, or multi-day offshore events (e.g., Fastnet, Sydney–Hobart, Round the Island).

### 4.3 Scenic routes (FFVoile-friendly cruising suggestions)

These are cruising areas in France suitable for FFVoile 3–4 level crews (calm waters, plenty of shelter, accessible ports):

- **Côte d'Azur / Îles de Lérins** — short hops between Cannes and Sainte-Marguerite; light winds, very protected.
- **Calanques de Marseille** — spectacular scenery, day sailing from Marseille's Vieux-Port; careful with Mistral wind.
- **Bassin d'Arcachon** — sheltered lagoon, ideal for Level 3; the Arguin sandbank and oyster ports are magical.
- **Île de Ré / Île d'Oléron** (Atlantic) — tidal but well-marked channels; sunny, varied anchorages.
- **Bretagne Sud** — Golfe du Morbihan, Belle-Île, Houat, Hoëdic — the heart of French cruising sailing; many FFVoile schools.
- **Bassin de Thau** — Mediterranean lagoon, steady wind, good for Level 2–3 day sails.
- **Corse (West coast)** — Calvi, Girolata, Scandola — dramatic scenery; exposed to westerlies, plan carefully.
- **Guadeloupe / Martinique** — the Caribbean playground for cruising FFVoile 4–5 sailors (warm, steady trades, clear water).

### 4.4 Social aspects

- **The "third hand" at the dock** — tradition of helping other sailors tie up, fendering off, or taking a line. Reciprocity is the social glue of the sailing community.
- **Apéro d'après-navigation** — the post-sailing drink at the clubhouse. Where stories are told, plans are made, and Level 1 students get demystified by veterans.
- **Club nights** — FFVoile clubs run weekly "permanences" (open evenings) with planning talks, repair workshops, guest speakers, slide shows of voyages.
- **Crew-finding**: many clubs run a board or a WhatsApp group for skippers looking for crew and vice-versa. Networking here is essential to Level 4 progression.
- **Racing crews** — joining a crew for weekend regattas is the fastest way to build experience and friendship.
- **Voyages** — clubs organize annual cruises (one week, two weeks, longer) to the destinations above. The social bonds built at sea are lifelong.
- **Mentorship**: FFVoile strongly encourages pairing experienced Level 5 skippers with Level 1–3 students; many clubs have a formal "parrainage" (mentoring) program.

### Practical teaching tips

- **Match the activity to the skill**: beginners love relay races and "blind sail" — competitive but forgiving. More advanced sailors love pursuit races because the handicap is built in.
- **Use games for drills**: turn a tacking drill into a race between boats; turn a sail-trim exercise into a "trim to the tell-tale" challenge.
- **Make regattas a class outing, not just an elite event**. Even Level 2 sailors benefit from participating in club races as crew.
- **Build the social side into the curriculum**: every FFVoile session should end with a short debrief on the dock, ideally over a drink. This is what turns students into long-term members.
- **For cruising routes**: pick destinations with **easy bailout** options (multiple ports within a few hours of sailing) for Level 3 outings.
- **Encourage cross-level crews**: a Level 5 skipper with a Level 2–3 crew is a classic FFVoile learning pattern that bonds the club.

## How to use this base in the article generator

The article generator should pull from this base by topic and FFVoile level. A daily article can be:

1. **A skill article** — focused on one technique, with FFVoile level called out (e.g., "Level 2 trim — easing the mainsheet to depower").
2. **A safety article** — short, action-oriented, ritual-driven (e.g., "The 30-second pre-departure PFD check").
3. **A weather article** — tied to current season, decode a Beaufort-scale observation in real life.
4. **A vocabulary article** — one term per day, in context, with a sketch or photo.
5. **A tactical article** — one term per day, in context, with a sketch or photo, focused on Regatta.

Suggested recurring structure:
- **Catchy title** (with the FFVoile level badge).
- **Why this matters** (1–2 sentences).
- **The essentials** (bulleted, scannable).
- **Common mistakes** (to keep it real and useful).
- **Source links** (for further reading — use the URLs above).
- **One practical drill** (a 5-minute activity to try).

This keeps each article short enough to be useful on a phone screen but rich enough to anchor a learner to the FFVoile progression.
